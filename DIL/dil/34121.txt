<div class="row result"><div class="col-sm-3 result_headword"><h3 headword_id="34121">P</h3><span class="citation">Cite this: eDIL s.v. P or <a class="reference" href="34121">dil.ie/34121</a></span><br/><span class="revision">Last Revised:
                2019</span><br/></div><div class="col-sm-9 result_content">
   
    <p class="result">
      <span class="trans">the <span class="def">thirteenth</span> <span class="def">letter</span> of the Irish alphabet, is not found in 
the early Ogham alphabet:</span> 
        ni bi p isin Gaedilc, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Auraic." title="eDIL Bibliography">Auraic.</strong></span> 1271
        
      , 
<span class="lbl">cf.</span> 
        4336
      , 
        4346
      . Indo-germanic <span class="frn ">p</span> when initial or between 
vowels disappeared in the Celtic languages. Consequently all 
words beginning with p in Old and Mid. Irish are derivatives 
from Latin, British, Romance, and occas. Scandinavian 
sources. In the oldest Irish loan-words from Latin (which 
came chiefly through a British medium), initial <i>p &gt; c (q)</i>, <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> 
cland, corcur, Cothraige (Irish name of St. Patrick) &lt; Lat. 
<span class="frn ">planta, purpur, Patricius</span>. When Ireland came into direct 
contact with Latin Christianity and culture, Latin <span class="frn ">p</span> remained 
in loan-words, and subsequently p was legitimized as an Irish 
letter and was called pin in the Ogham alphabet, 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Auraic." title="eDIL Bibliography">Auraic.</strong></span> 1365
      .</p>
   
    <p class="result">The account given of p in the 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Auraic." title="eDIL Bibliography">Auraicept</strong></span> 1269 fg.
      , 
        4335 fg.
       
is obscure; apparently it was regarded as the softening (bocad, 
bogad, a term expld. in Gloss. as unvoicing) of b (acc. to 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Auraic." title="eDIL Bibliography">Auraic.</strong></span> 
4347
      , some Latin grammarians held that bh was written for p, 
a view denied by others). Later it received the name peith or 
peith bhog (
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/P.OC." title="eDIL Bibliography">P. O'C.</strong></span>
      , 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/OR.Poems" title="eDIL Bibliography">O'R. Poems</strong></span>
      .); O'Reilly explains peith as = 
beith(e), name of the birch-tree and in Ogham of the letter b, 
hence peith bhog = `<span class="quote">soft b</span>'. (In Hogan Luibh. and 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Dinn." title="eDIL Bibliography">Dinneen</strong></span>
      , 
peith = <span class="ediltr">dwarf elder</span>.)</p>
   
    <p class="result">In early Irish orthography the letter p, when initial and in 
the groups mp, rp, and pp, stands for the voiceless labial 
explosive p; medial between vowels it generally represents 
the voiced labial b which replaces it in later notation (O.Ir. 
opair = mod. obair). Late Mid.Ir. scribes occas. use p incorrectly to express medial or final lenited b, <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> gapaid, ro 
gap = gabaid (gaibid), ro gab.</p>
   
    <p class="result">In lenition p becomes `f, in early MSS. often written ph 
(occas. fp). In O.Ir. lenition of p may have been optional, 
see 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Thurn." title="eDIL Bibliography">Thurn. Hdb.</strong></span> § 231
      , 
        5
      .</p>
   
    <p class="result">Initial p and b somet. interchange; for exx. see <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=2 pell">2 pell</a></span>, 
<span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=bellic">bellic</a></span>, <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=p&#xED;ast">píast</a></span>, <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=pla(e)">pla(e)</a></span>, <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=plaesc">plaesc</a></span>, <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=poc(c)">poc(c)</a></span>, <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=pr&#xE1;ca">práca</a></span>. 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/P.OC." title="eDIL Bibliography">P. O'C.</strong></span>
       gives 
several words beginning either with b or p, the latter form 
being colloquial (`<span class="quote">vulgo</span>').</p>
   
    <p class="result">Initial p also interchanges somet. with f in Mid.Ir. and 
later; in some cases p is the older form (see <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=pailm">pailm</a></span>, <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=pairche">pairche</a></span>, 
<span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=p&#xED;t">pít</a></span>, <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=promaid">promaid</a></span>), in others f (<span class="lbl">e.g.</span> O.Ir. fetarlicce &gt; Mid.Ir. 
petarlaicc).</p>
   
    <p class="result">Irish words beginning with p are prob. all originally loan-words; those of the oldest period are borrowed mainly from 
Latin, those of later times from Romance sources through the 
medium at first (from the end of the 12th to the latter part of 
the 14th cent.) of Anglo-French, later of English.</p>
