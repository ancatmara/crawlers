<div class="row result"><div class="col-sm-3 result_headword"><h3 headword_id="31187">maccu, moccu</h3><span class="citation">Cite this: eDIL s.v. maccu, moccu or <a class="reference" href="31187">dil.ie/31187</a></span><br/><span class="revision">Last Revised:
                2019</span><br/><p style="margin-top: 4px" class="small">Forms:
                moccu, mocu, maccu, macu, maccu, maccu, macu, maccu, mocui</p></div><div class="col-sm-9 result_content">
   


    <p class="result">
      <span class="pos subst." style="color: #ff8000">subst.</span> <span class="itype invar.">invar.</span> See 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/GuidetoOgam" title="eDIL Bibliography">A Guide to Ogam</strong></span> 119-20
      , 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/&#xC9;igse" title="eDIL Bibliography">Éigse</strong></span> xxviii 44, 50-1
      . A word belonging solely to the 
O.Ir. period, always used in apposition to a preceding npr. 
(occasionally a place-name) and folld. by another npr. in gen. 
case. Acc. to MacNeill, 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/&#xC9;riu" title="eDIL Bibliography">Ériu</strong></span> iii 42 fg
      . the combination moccu 
+ gen. is equivalent to `<span class="trans">
        <span class="def">belonging to</span> the <i>gens</i> or <span class="def">family</span> of</span>', 
the follg. gen. being the name of the eponymous ancestor; in 
Adamnán's Vita Columbae it is frequently preceded by the 
abl. <span class="frn ">gente</span> and possibly its use in Irish after a npr. involves an 
ellipse. The latest contemporary use in AU is in the year 690, 
the last instance in 789 (
        <span class="frn ">comotatio</span> . . . Mochuae micu 
[= macu] Lugedon). Latest contemporary use is in 9th century in Mart. Óeng (see 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/&#xC9;igse" title="eDIL Bibliography">Éigse</strong></span> xxviii 43-44
      ). The oldest form may be mocu, frequent in Adamnán; maccu is usual in L Ardm., macu and 
 
maccu in AU; generally written with contraction m̅c̅. In the 
Mid.Ir. period the word was often changed by writers who no 
longer understood its exact meaning into mac uí, mac úa 
(common forms in Mid.Ir. MSS., see <span class="frn ">variae lectiones</span> in Fél.) 
and by mod. edd. it is often printed as two words (so nearly 
always in AU); Marianus Scotus translates it by <span class="frn ">filius nepotis</span> 
(
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/ToddLect." title="eDIL Bibliography">Todd Lect.</strong></span> iii 17.17
      ).</p>
