        <p class="result">(<span class="edilb">a</span>) <span class="trans">
            <i>the</i> <span class="def">seat</span> <i>of</i> <span class="def">honour</span> in a <span class="def">king's</span> 
or <span class="def">chief's</span> <span class="def">hall</span>;</span> perhaps an earlier use than the follg., pointing to a primitive arrangement like that in the old Icelandic 
house, where the seat of honour was in the middle of the 
northern bench facing south. <span class="lbl">Cf.</span> 
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Laws" title="eDIL Bibliography">Laws</strong></span> iv 338.15
           (describing arrangement of a king's banqueting-hall): cuslennaigh 
... a n-airt[h]iur ḟoitsi. isinn leth eliu a fochlu fénnid 
`<span class="quote">his champion's seat</span>' (`<span class="quote">on the other side, in the north, a man 
at arms</span>', MacNeill 
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/PRIA" title="eDIL Bibliography">PRIA</strong></span> xxxvi C 16. 306
          , see 
            n
          . <span class="lbl">ib.</span>; 
Plummer suggests reading: isin leth chliu a fochlu feinnid[a] `<span class="quote">on the left is his champion's seat</span> (MS. notes <span class="lbl">s.v.</span>). 
Binchy, 
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Cr&#xED;thG." title="eDIL Bibliography">Críth G.</strong></span> 591
          , prints: ailiu, i fochlu, which agrees 
with MacNeill's rendering, but compare the exx. of f.<span class="oRef">¤</span> 
feinneda below). i nairt[h]iur fochlai `<span class="quote">on the east side of 
the champion's seat</span>' <span class="lbl">ib.</span> (= 
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Cr&#xED;thG." title="eDIL Bibliography">Críth G.</strong></span> 597

In older literature, however, the name seems to have been

<span class="def">champion</span> or chief <span class="def">man</span> <span class="def">at arms</span>;</span> hence generally called f.<span class="oRef">¤</span>

            [f]ochla (féinneadh),
