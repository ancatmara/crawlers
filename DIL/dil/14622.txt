<div class="row result"><div class="col-sm-3 result_headword"><h3 headword_id="14622">dantmír</h3><span class="citation">Cite this: eDIL s.v. dantmír or <a class="reference" href="14622">dil.ie/14622</a></span><br/><span class="revision">Last Revised:
                2019</span><br/><p style="margin-top: 4px" class="small">Forms:
                dant-mír, dant-mír</p></div><div class="col-sm-9 result_content">
   
    <p class="result">
      <span class="pos n" style="color: #ff8000">n</span> 
      <span class="itype nn">nn</span>, <span class="gen n.">n.</span> (<span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=dant">dant</a></span> + <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=m&#xED;r">mír</a></span>). Usually identified with curad-mír `the heroes' morsel,' but dant-mír <span class="trans">seems to signify a piece of 
food which, according to old custom, was put between the 
teeth of the dead:</span> rosfúair hi fástig oc fuiniu héisc for 
indéin ┐ bae cenn Lomnai for bir hi cinn na tened. in 
cétlucht doralad dind indéin rantai Coirpri doa tríb nonburaib ┐ ní tardad dantmír i mbeolu in chinn olṡodain ba 
geis la Fiannu <span class="ediltr">the first batch that was taken from the 
gridiron, Coirpre distributes it to his thrice nine men; but 
the `<span class="frn ">dantmír</span>' was not put into the mouth of the head 
though it was a `<span class="frn ">geis</span>' with the ancients (to do so)</span> (rather: '<span class="quote">a thing which it was a <span class="frn ">geis</span> with the <span class="frn ">Fíanna</span> to do</span>' 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/RC" title="eDIL Bibliography">RC</strong></span> xxxvii 19) 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Corm.Bodl." title="eDIL Bibliography">Corm. Bodl.</strong></span> 30. 2
      . Stokes's interpolation is wrong and disturbs 
the sense. The custom must have been deeply rooted, for 
in the old Egerton fragment of Finn's death, 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/ZCP" title="eDIL Bibliography">ZCP</strong></span> i 464 <span class="lbl">sq.</span>
      , 
it is told how supernatural powers secure the dant-mír for 
the decapitated head of Finn: confuaradar iascaire na 
Boinde. ceathrar dóibh .i. trímaic Uircreann ┐ Aicleach... 
conécmaing Aicleach a cheann de ┐ corubhradar maic U.— 
rucsat a chenn leo i ḟásteach ┐ roḟuinsit a niasc ┐ rorannsat i nde. a cheann hi cind tenedh. tabraid dantmír dó 
or fer dubh docluichi ó na mair Aicleach. rorannadh in 
tiasc i nde .i. fo thrí ┐ badar trí cuibhrind ann béos. cidh 
so or fer díbh. is ann isbert an cend a cind tened:</p>
  
  
    <p class="result"> 
ised fodera an tresraind libhsi cen síl napeli
    </p>
  
  
    <p class="result"> 
arnatabhar damsa oc proind uaibsi mo ṁír ma...ele.</p>
  
  
    <p class="result"> 
<span class="trans">The Brehon Laws punished the removal of the `<span class="frn ">dant-mír</span>' with `<span class="frn ">athgabáil treise</span>':</span> athgabáil treise i folomrad 
do mairb (<span class="case d">d</span>
      <span class="number s.">s.</span> <span class="gen fem">fem</span>.)...im archor auptha mimir do chor do 
choin dantmir do breith ó fir besa ái <span class="ediltr">carrying away the 
`<span class="frn ">dantmír</span>' from the person to whom it belongs</span> 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Laws" title="eDIL Bibliography">Laws</strong></span> i 176.
4
      ; to which the commentary adds the following note: 
        .i. 
curadmír .i. do breith ón fir isa hae hé .i. diablad in 
cura[d]mír no eneclann .i. amail roberta ó Choinculainn. 
eneclann and ar treisi, 
          <span class="lbl">ib.</span> 180. 3 <span class="lbl">f.b.</span>
        
       This seems only an 
attempt of the commentator to find some sense in a word 
that naturally enough was obscure to him, as the pagan 
custom it refers to was bound to have disappeared with 
Christianity. </p>
