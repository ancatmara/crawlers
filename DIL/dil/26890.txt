<div class="row result"><div class="col-sm-3 result_headword"><h3 headword_id="26890">I</h3><span class="citation">Cite this: eDIL s.v. I or <a class="reference" href="26890">dil.ie/26890</a></span><br/><p style="margin-top: 4px" class="small">Forms:
                ida, ĭ, ī, e</p></div><div class="col-sm-9 result_content">
   
    <p class="result">eighth letter of the Irish alphabet, stood fifth in the vowel-group of the Ogham alphabet and was known by the 
name idad `<span class="trans">
        <span class="def">yew</span>
      </span>,' 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Auraic." title="eDIL Bibliography">Auraic.</strong></span> 5593.
       
        ida, 
          5523
        
      . 
        is o idhedh 
doinsgain ip[h]in, 
          4864
        
      . 
        fer didiu ... idad inna selbad ┐ ina 
thoghairm, 
          1676
        
      . 
        ioghadh, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/IGT" title="eDIL Bibliography">IGT</strong></span> Introd. § 9
        
      , where it and u 
are described as impure vowels `ar son go ttiaguid a mbrígh 
chonnsuineadh'. The writer of the tracts is thinking in 
terms of the Latin vowels, as he instances <span class="frn ">uóbís</span> and <span class="frn ">ihuinnéis</span> (read <span class="frn ">iuuenis</span> ?, Ed.). There is no evidence of the 
existence of consonantal <i>i</i> in Old Irish. Any diphthong beginning with <i>i</i> was regarded as a single letter in the 
Ogham alphabet and was called pin or ifin (iphin), 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Auraic." title="eDIL Bibliography">Auraic.</strong></span> 
5611
      , 
        5524
      -
        5
      . 
        iphin a fidh ina reimim .i. don fir, 
          4862
        
      .</p>
  
  
    <p class="result"> 
The paragraph refs. below are to 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/GOI" title="eDIL Bibliography">GOI</strong></span>
      
    </p>
   
    <p class="result"> 
O.Ir. ĭ in stressed syllables may represent (a) IE <span class="frn ">ĭ</span>: fir, 
 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/GOI" title="eDIL Bibliography">GOI</strong></span> (§ 57) ; (b) infected IE <span class="frn ">e</span> : mil (Gk. <span class="frn ">μέλι</span>), —biur 
(
        § 75
      ). The syllables : ri, li, in, usually represent 
IE <span class="frn ">r̥ l̥ n̥</span>: riuth, mlith, bind (
        § 214
      -
        §215
      ).</p>
  
  
    <p class="result"> 
O.Ir. ī in stressed syllables may represent (a) IE <span class="frn ">ī</span> : lí (Lat. 
<span class="frn ">līuor</span>), rím (Lat. <span class="frn ">rītus</span>) ; (b) IE <span class="frn ">ē</span> : rí (Lat. <span class="frn ">rēx</span>), lín (Lat. 
<span class="frn ">plēnus</span>) (
        § 58
      ).</p>
  
  
    <p class="result"> 
In unstressed syllables <i>ĭ</i> is the regular vowel between 
two palatal consonants belonging to the same syllable : 
berid, suidigthir, timthirecht (
        § 103
      ).</p>
  
  
    <p class="result"> 
<i>ĭ</i> may also occur in a stressed syllable as a glide to denote 
the palatal quality of a following consonant, especially 
when the latter ends the word : maith, clainde, guidid, 
láim; and in Wb. is sometimes written before a non-palatal 
consonant after a <i>ē</i> which arises out of the compensatory 
lengthening of <i>e</i> : céitbuid (
        § 54
      ).</p>
  
  
    <p class="result"> 
Beside <i>éu</i>, <i>éui</i> (or <i>éoi</i>) of ds. and gs. before <i>l r n</i>, <i>íu</i> and 
<i>íui</i> sometimes occur : ceníul, ceníuil, éiuin, féiuir (
        § 55
      ).</p>
  
  
    <p class="result"> 
The diphthong <i>aí</i>, <i>oí</i> may also be written <i>oe</i>, <i>ae</i> (`<span class="quote">almost 
only when beginning a word in Wb.</span>', 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/LewisandPed." title="eDIL Bibliography">Lewis and Ped.</strong></span> § 14
      ). 
For its origin see 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/GOI" title="eDIL Bibliography">GOI</strong></span> § 67.
      
    </p>
  
  
    <p class="result"> 
The diphthong <i>ía</i> (to be distinguished from <i>ïa</i> as in 
grian `<span class="ediltr">sand, gravel</span>') may represent (a) arch. O.Ir. <span class="frn ">é</span> : 
-tíagat (<span class="lbl">cf.</span> 
        -tēgot, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Cambr." title="eDIL Bibliography">Cambr.</strong></span> 38b
          
        
      ), fíada (<span class="lbl">cf.</span> fēdot (gs.) 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Cambr." title="eDIL Bibliography">Cambr.</strong></span> 
37c
        
      ) ; (b) Lat. <span class="frn ">ē</span> or <span class="frn ">oe</span>: fíal `<span class="quote">uēlum</span>,' pían `<span class="quote">poena</span>' 
        § 53
      ).</p>
  
  
    <p class="result"> 
In Mid. Ir. final unstressed <span class="frn ">i</span> and e fell together in pronunciation and were often confused in orthography.</p>
