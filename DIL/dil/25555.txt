      <p class="result"> 
(<span class="edilb">a</span>) <span class="trans">
          <span class="def">prohibited act</span> or <span class="def">person</span>
        </span>, <span class="trans">a <span class="def">tabu</span>, a <span class="def">prohibition</span>
        </span>, the infraction (`coll' `milliud') 
of which involved disastrous consequences. Sometimes 
these `gessi' were fortuitous, originating in the fact 
that a certain course of action had produced disastrous 
results in the past, sometimes they were rules of honour or 
prudent abstention, often they rested on deep-seated primitive beliefs and ideas. Such tabus might be attached to a 
rank or office, <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> the tabus laid on the king of Tara and on 
some of the provincial kings, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Leb.Cert." title="eDIL Bibliography">Leb. Cert.</strong></span> 2
        -
          24
         (where they are also 
called urgartha `<span class="quote">things forbidden</span>,' and opposed to buada 
= ada `<span class="quote">lucky things</span>' or `<span class="quote">things prescribed</span>'). <span class="lbl">Cf.</span> for opposition 
of buada and 
          geasa, 
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/HyMaine" title="eDIL Bibliography">Hy Maine</strong></span> 13.21
          
        , and of ada and 
          geis, 

            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/CCath." title="eDIL Bibliography">CCath.</strong></span> 1166
          
        . See also Macalister, Temair Breg; Baudiš, 

          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/&#xC9;riu" title="eDIL Bibliography">Ériu</strong></span> viii 104 
          <span class="lbl">seq.</span>
         Frazer, <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/GoldenBough" title="eDIL Bibliography">Golden Bough</strong></span>, ch. xxvii § 1 
(abbr. ed.), 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Celtica" title="eDIL Bibliography">Celtica</strong></span> xxiii 38-59
        . They might bind a whole community, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/TBC-LL1" title="eDIL Bibliography">TBC-LL¹</strong></span> 
1190
        ; 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/RC" title="eDIL Bibliography">RC</strong></span> viii 56.12
        . More often they were restrictions 
on an individual, either owing to the predictions of druids 
or soothsayers that certain actions would bring ill-luck, or 
imposed on him by another who had power to punish their 
violation, or who trusted to his sense of honour or his fear. 
For a list of Cú Chulainn's gessi see 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/RC" title="eDIL Bibliography">RC</strong></span> xiv 398.
