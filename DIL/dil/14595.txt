 <span class="edilb">I</span> <span class="trans">A <span class="def">Dane</span>, a man from <span class="def">Denmark</span>, a <span class="frn ">dubgall</span> or `<span class="def">dark 
foreigner</span>' as opposed to a <span class="frn ">findgall</span> or <span class="frn ">lochlannach</span> `a 
<span class="def">Norwegian</span>'; also <span class="frn ">dub-danar</span> under the influence of <span class="frn ">dub-gall</span> and <span class="frn ">dub-geinnti</span>; but <span class="frn ">find-danar</span> does not occur. The 
 <span class="number pl.">pl.</span> Danair is never used for `<span class="ediltr">Denmark</span>
        </span>' (<span class="lbl">cf.</span> <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=Danmairg">Danmairg</a></span>). 
Other synon. danmarcach (anmarcach), <span class="frn ">daunites</span> (aunites). 
—In the Annals of Ulster, which offer only three instances, 
danar appears for the first time under the year 985; then 
it appears once in the SR and in various early Mid.-Ir. 
texts from the LL and YBL. In all probability the name 
became fixed about the middle of the 10th century; it is later 
than the synonymous <span class="frn ">dubgall</span> and <span class="frn ">dubgeinnti</span> (first occurrence

 <span class="edilb">V</span> <span class="trans">In depreciatory sense a <span class="def">cruel</span> and <span class="def">ferocious</span> <span class="def">foreigner</span> 
or <span class="def">barbarian</span>, a <span class="def">robber</span>, <span class="def">pirate</span>, <span class="def">bandit</span>
        </span>; often connected with 
<span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=allmarach">allmarach</a></span>, <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=d&#xED;bergach">díbergach</a></span>, <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=indarbthach">indarbthach</a></span>; synon. <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=2 anmargach">2 anmargach</a></span>.

has taken place also in Old Norse <span class="frn ">Brjótr berg-dana</span> `<span class="quote">the
