<div class="row result"><div class="col-sm-3 result_headword"><h3 headword_id="14665">dardóin</h3><span class="citation">Cite this: eDIL s.v. dardóin or <a class="reference" href="14665">dil.ie/14665</a></span><br/><span class="revision">Last Revised:
                2019</span><br/><p style="margin-top: 4px" class="small">Forms:
                dardáin, cétáine, cétáin</p></div><div class="col-sm-9 result_content">
   


    <p class="result">
      <span class="pos n" style="color: #ff8000">n</span>  dardáin, <span class="itype indecl">indecl</span>. <span class="trans">
        <span class="def">Thursday</span>
      </span> from *etar-dá-óin 
 <span class="ediltr">between two fasts</span>, i.e. between cét-áin and áin diden; hence it never, like cét-áin (<span class="case g">g</span>
      <span class="number s.">s.</span> cétáine, cétáin), forms 
a <span class="case g">g</span>
      <span class="number s.">s.</span> in -áine, dia dardáin being the constant form from the earliest occurrences up to the present day. The accent falls on the ultimate, comp. Bodl. ACC, 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/RC" title="eDIL Bibliography">RC</strong></span> xx 164.1
      : teged Colomb cundáil cáin: i teg a ríg cech dardáin. 
Though dardáin does not seem to occur in Old-Ir. MSS, 
its appearance in such old texts as the ACC and the Brehon 
Laws is sufficient proof of its Old-Ir. origin. The e of the 
unaccented `etar' would naturally assimilate itself to a 
preceding dé or dia, possibly as early as the early 8th century 
when dia was still dé.</p>
