<div class="row result"><div class="col-sm-3 result_headword"><h3 headword_id="33175">nícon</h3><span class="citation">Cite this: eDIL s.v. nícon or <a class="reference" href="33175">dil.ie/33175</a></span><br/><span class="revision">Last Revised:
                2019</span><br/><p style="margin-top: 4px" class="small">Forms:
                nícon, níco, noco, nocho (n-), nocha (n-), nocha, nochan, nacon, nícon, Nícon, nocon, nochon</p></div><div class="col-sm-9 result_content">
   
    <p class="result">
      <span class="pos adv." style="color: #ff8000">adv.</span> of negation, a strengthened form of ní, used before 
vbs. The second element -con is expld. as perfective particle <span class="frn ">com- (con-)</span> 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/&#xC9;tudesCelt." title="eDIL Bibliography">Études Celt.</strong></span> xv 505
      , 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/AktenMarburg" title="eDIL Bibliography">Akten Marburg</strong></span> 112-13
       but see 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/&#xC9;riu" title="eDIL Bibliography">Ériu</strong></span> xxi 90-92
      . Used at the 
beginning of a main clause with the indic. (or potential or 
optative subj.), folld. by the conjunct form of the vb. In the 
Glosses, the particle -con is always written in conjunction 
with the vb., ní either standing as a separate word or joined 
to it. The initial consonant of the vb. is lenited (not always 
expressed in MSS.).</p>
   
    <p class="result"> 
In Mid.Ir. the form nícon somet. becomes níco before a 
consonant, and is early weakened to noco (noco n- before a 
vowel or lenited f); later with lenited c, nocho (n-), nocha (n-), 
which prevail in later Mid.Ir.; early Mod.Ir. nocha (generally 
eclipsing follg. cons., nochan before a vowel and lenited f), 
still persisting in North Ireland and in Scottish Gaelic in the 
neg. <span class="frn ">cha</span>, <span class="frn ">chan</span>.</p>
   
    <p class="result"> 
In O.Ir. nacon replaces nícon in dependent clauses; this 
construction disappears in Mid.Ir.</p>
   
    <p class="result"> 
Nícon (nocon, nochon) expresses a more emphatic denial 
than ní alone (in later usage there is not much difference).</p>
