<div class="row result"><div class="col-sm-3 result_headword"><h3 headword_id="14791">5 de</h3><span class="citation">Cite this: eDIL s.v. 5 de or <a class="reference" href="14791">dil.ie/14791</a></span><br/><span class="revision">Last Revised:
                2019</span><br/><p style="margin-top: 4px" class="small">Forms:
                de, dé, dæ, de, -de</p></div><div class="col-sm-9 result_content">
   
    <p class="result">
      <span class="pos prep" style="color: #ff8000">prep</span> 
      -te, -ti added to comparatives, corresponding in 
sense to Lat. <span class="frn ">eo</span>, Eng. <span class="frn ">the</span>. Identical with <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=1 de">1 de, di</a></span> <span class="trans">
        <span class="def">from</span> it, 
<span class="def">because</span> of it</span>. In Oir. still treated as an independent word 
and always written de (
        dé, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Wb." title="eDIL Bibliography">Wb.</strong></span> 22 d 2
        
      ; 
        dæ, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Ml." title="eDIL Bibliography">Ml.</strong></span> 90 a 11
        
      
= de); in Mid.-Ir. wholly enclitic and written together 
with the comparative, the unaspirated, now intervocalic, -d- 
according to the rules of spelling being written -t-: <span class="frn ">córaite</span> 
(Oir. <span class="frn ">córu de</span>). -d slender at all phases of the language 
and never discarded (Quiggin's explanation of `a chách,' 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/QuigginVorton.W&#xF6;rter" title="eDIL Bibliography">Quiggin Vorton. Wörter</strong></span> 34
      , is wrong). Its place is immediately after the 
comparative, only exceptionally separated from it by an 
enclitic word as 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Ml." title="eDIL Bibliography">Ml.</strong></span> 23 d 23
      : [im]mi ardu ni de; 
        <span class="lbl">ib.</span> 83 b 11
      : 
bid étrummu doib de.
    </p>
   
    
      <p class="result"> 
In certain points the Mid.-Ir. usage diverges from Oir. 
In the Glosses -de is used proleptically, referring in a 
general way to a cause which follows introduced by the 
prep. di or trí, <span class="lbl">e.g.</span>:</p>
      

      
        <p class="result">
          <span class="edilb">A</span> robtar erdarcu de dind ulc 
doratad forru <span class="ediltr">they were the more famous for the evil that 
was inflicted on them</span> 
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Ml." title="eDIL Bibliography">Ml.</strong></span> 47 a 18
          . airmi ardu ni de tri 
dul isna lucu arda <span class="ediltr">for we are the higher for going into high 
places</span> 
            <span class="lbl">ib.</span> 23 d 23
          . 
            is huilliu de mo ḟreiscsiu do (= di) neuch 
rorádis su, 
              <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Thes." title="eDIL Bibliography">Thes.</strong></span> ii 2. 34
            
          .</p>
      
    . 
      
        <p class="result">—<span class="edilb">B</span> (di, tri + vn. + gen.) conid 
étrummu dæ in bochtae...tri frescissin messa fíriéin 
ind ríg doib iarum <span class="ediltr">so that the poverty is the lighter through 
their expectation of the just judgment of the king afterwards</span> 
 
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Ml." title="eDIL Bibliography">Ml.</strong></span> 90 a 11
          . is moltaidiu de tri tabairt fortachtan cid du 
neuch nachidáirilli <span class="ediltr">it is the more praiseworthy through 
giving help even to one who does not deserve it</span> 
            <span class="lbl">ib.</span> 84 c 13
          . 
(
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/LU" title="eDIL Bibliography">LU</strong></span> 60 a 38
           fri seems used in a similar way: rafetammár 
ém in gilla sin ┐ ní messaite fria fis is dalta dún.)</p>

MOD. Suffixed to any adjective: ní folláinede madra 
marbh é bheith san uisge glan. Ní milsede an domblas é 
bheith i nárthach airgid. Níor ṁeasa do dhuine rud do 
ghearrfadh a sgórnach ná sgian óir. Aran gcuma gcéadna 
ní folláinede ┐ ní milsede ┐ ní macántaide sgéal neaṁcháidh 
é bheith dá innsint i ngaedhilg uasail, P. O'Leary 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/GJ" title="eDIL Bibliography">GJ</strong></span> vi 110
      . 
Other comparatives from the mod. literary period are 
deiside, gilide, giorraide, liaide, siaide, teoide, usaide. 
—But in the spoken language usually confined to a small 
number of adjectives. Very common are fearrde, miste, 
móide (<span class="lbl">q.v.</span>), idiomatically used with the copula and the 
prepositions la and do. The original sense has been 
obscured and as a living comparative particle -de seems 
obsolete.</p>
