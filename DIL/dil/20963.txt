<div class="row result"><div class="col-sm-3 result_headword"><h3 headword_id="20963">F</h3><span class="citation">Cite this: eDIL s.v. F or <a class="reference" href="20963">dil.ie/20963</a></span><br/><p style="margin-top: 4px" class="small">Forms:
                f, f, -h-, -βf, f/p, β, f-, ḟ, fh, ḟh, s, ff, ḟ, ff, ḟf, bf, bḟ, bhf, fh</p></div><div class="col-sm-9 result_content">
   
    <p class="result">the third consonant of the Ogham alphabet, called in 
Irish after the <span class="trans">
        <span class="def">alder-tree</span>
      </span>, fern (
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Auraic." title="eDIL Bibliography">Auraic.</strong></span> 1168
      , 
        4264
      , 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/IGT" title="eDIL Bibliography">IGT</strong></span>, 
Introd. § 4
      ), represents a bilabial voiceless spirant. O. Ir. 
initial f in most cases comes from IE <span class="frn ">ṷ</span>, <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> fer, corresponding to Lat. <span class="frn ">vir</span>, W. <span class="frn ">gwr</span>; fír: Lat. <span class="frn ">uērus</span>, W. <span class="frn ">gwir</span>. Both 
initially and in the interior of words it may also denote 
lenited <i>s</i> &lt; <span class="frn ">sṷ</span>, <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> (mo) fiur (siur &lt; *<span class="frn ">sṷesōr</span>, W. <span class="frn ">chwaer</span>); 
(mór) fesser (sesser; <span class="lbl">cf.</span> W. <span class="frn ">chwech</span>); airfitiud, &lt; *<span class="frn ">air-sétiud</span> (<span class="frn ">√ sṷeizd</span>); toffunn, vn. of <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=do-seinn">do-seinn</a></span>. In the later 
language this f lenites to -h-. Occasionally <i>f</i> seems written 
for -βf, <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> atrefea, fut. of <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=ad-treba">ad-treba</a></span>, 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Wb." title="eDIL Bibliography">Wb.</strong></span> 30b18
      , 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Ml." title="eDIL Bibliography">Ml.</strong></span> 36a19
      , 

        107a15
      . In loan words <i>f</i> may replace Lat. <span class="frn ">u</span>, <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> fín 
&lt; <span class="frn ">uinum</span>, fís &lt; <span class="frn ">visio</span>; Lat. <span class="frn ">ph</span>, <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> felsub. fellsam &lt; <span class="frn ">philosoph</span>-; 
Lat. <span class="frn ">f</span>, <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> figor &lt; <span class="frn ">figura</span>. In fromad beside promad (Lat. 
<span class="frn ">prob-</span>) there is an alternation of f/p also found in a few 
other words, native as well as borrowed, <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> fairche /pairche 
(&lt; <span class="frn ">parūchia</span>), fairisínech, pairisínech, frém /prém (O. Ir. <span class="frn ">frén</span>). 
In late Romance loanwords <i>f</i> may replace <i>p</i>. <span class="lbl">eg.</span> falafraigh &lt; <span class="frn ">palefrei</span>. In Mid. Ir. rotfia = <span class="frn ">rotbia</span> (<span class="lbl">e.g.</span> 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/LL" title="eDIL Bibliography">LL</strong></span> 306b13
      , 
<span class="frn ">ratfia</span>, 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Lism.L." title="eDIL Bibliography">Lism. L.</strong></span> 350
      , 
        464
      , <span class="lbl">etc.</span>) <i>f</i> may represent the sound of β 
unvoiced by preceding, t, but <span class="lbl">cf.</span> 
        ronfia, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Lism.L." title="eDIL Bibliography">Lism. L.</strong></span> 775
        
      . </p>
   
    
      <p class="result"> 
Inorganic <i>f</i> is frequently inserted</p>
     
      
        <p class="result"> <span class="edilb">(a)</span> in O. Ir. deuterotonic forms of compound verbs before the initial vowel 
of a stressed syllable, <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> do-fuarat `<span class="ediltr">remains over</span>' (*<span class="frn ">di-uss-reth</span>), do-fuissim `<span class="ediltr">generates</span>' (*<span class="frn ">to-uss-sem</span>). </p>
        
      
        <p class="result">
          <span class="edilb">(b)</span> In 
Mid. Ir. it also appears in forms such as <span class="frn ">do-fongad</span> (see 
<span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=tongaid">tongaid</a></span>), <span class="frn ">do-fuit</span> = <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=do-tuit">do-tuit</a></span>, <span class="frn ">do-fargaid</span> (targaid `<span class="ediltr">offered</span>') 
<span class="frn ">do-fucc</span> = doficc, and <span class="lbl">cf.</span> dafiṅggebad `<span class="ediltr">who would ward him 
off</span>' 
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/SR" title="eDIL Bibliography">SR</strong></span> 5796
          , 
            5820
          . And occasionally in a hiatus form of 
a simple verb: bífad, sec. fut. of <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=benaid">benaid</a></span> 
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/SR" title="eDIL Bibliography">SR</strong></span> 5812
          . bífed 

            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/LL" title="eDIL Bibliography">LL</strong></span> 60.28
          . <span class="lbl">Cf.</span> Fer fī = Fer hí, <span class="lbl">s.v.</span> <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=3 e&#xF3;">3 eó</a></span>.</p>
        
      
        <p class="result">
          <span class="edilb">(c)</span> In a number of 
words which in O. Ir. began with a vowel <i>f</i> is prefixed in 
the later language. In the classical period both forms are 
in use in the literature, but it is in most cases the f- form 
that has survived in Mod. Ir.: <span class="frn ">fadaig-</span> <span class="ediltr">kindle</span> (O. Ir. <span class="frn ">ad-doí</span>); <span class="frn ">faicsin</span> <span class="ediltr">seeing</span> (O. Ir. <span class="frn ">aicsiu</span>); <span class="frn ">faidlenn</span> <span class="ediltr">rack</span> (earlier 
<span class="frn ">aidlenn</span>); <span class="frn ">faire</span> <span class="ediltr">watch, heed</span> (O. Ir. <span class="frn ">aire</span>); <span class="frn ">faisnéis</span> <span class="ediltr">relating</span> (O. 
Ir. <span class="frn ">aisndís</span>); <span class="frn ">fanaid</span> <span class="ediltr">waits</span> (O. Ir. <span class="frn ">anaid</span>); <span class="frn ">farrad</span> in phr. <span class="frn ">i 
bhf.</span>
          <span class="oRef">¤</span> (O. Ir. <span class="frn ">arrad</span>); <span class="frn ">fás</span> <span class="ediltr">growing</span> (O. Ir. <span class="frn ">ás</span>); <span class="frn ">faslach</span> (<span class="frn ">aslach</span>); 
<span class="frn ">fastad</span>, -ód, -úd (O. Ir. <span class="frn ">astud</span>); <span class="frn ">fathach</span> <span class="ediltr">giant</span> (<span class="frn ">athach</span>, O. Ir. 
<span class="frn ">aithech</span> <span class="ediltr">peasant</span>); <span class="frn ">fatód</span> <span class="ediltr">kindling</span> (O. Ir. <span class="frn ">atúd</span>); <span class="frn ">féta-</span> <span class="ediltr">is 
able</span> (O. Ir. <span class="frn ">-éta</span> <span class="ediltr">possesses</span>); <span class="frn ">fiafraig-</span> <span class="ediltr">inquire</span> (O. Ir. <span class="frn ">-iarfaig</span>); 
<span class="frn ">focus</span> <span class="ediltr">near, neighbourhood</span> (O. Ir. <span class="frn ">ocus</span>); <span class="frn ">fúacht</span> <span class="ediltr">cold</span> (O. Ir. 
<span class="frn ">úacht</span>); <span class="frn ">fúaigid</span> (<span class="lbl">cf.</span> <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=&#xFA;aigid">úaigid</a></span>); <span class="frn ">fúar</span> <span class="ediltr">cold</span> (O. Ir. <span class="frn ">úar</span>); <span class="frn ">fúath</span> 
 <span class="ediltr">horror</span> (O. Ir. <span class="frn ">úath</span>); <span class="frn ">fuinnsenn</span> gs., <span class="ediltr">ashtree</span>, (see <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=uinnius">uinnius</a></span>); 
<span class="frn ">fuiseóc</span> <span class="ediltr">lark</span> (see <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=uise&#xF3;g">uiseóg</a></span>); <span class="frn ">furnaide</span> <span class="ediltr">waiting</span> (O. Ir. <span class="frn ">irnaide</span>, 
<span class="frn ">ernaide</span>; see <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=airnaide">airnaide</a></span>).</p>
       
  
  
    <p class="result"> 
In the case of certain words, mostly verbal forms, beginning with <i>f</i> followed by a vowel, a form without the initial <i>f</i> 
is also recognized in the Early Modern period; thus we 
get, <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> <span class="frn ">fácbáil</span>, <span class="frn ">ácbáil</span>; <span class="frn ">faghbháil</span>, <span class="frn ">aghbháil</span>; <span class="frn ">fúair</span>, <span class="frn ">úair</span>; 
<span class="frn ">furáilemh</span>, <span class="frn ">uráilemh</span>; <span class="frn ">falafraigh</span>, <span class="frn ">alafruigh</span>; <span class="frn ">feiretrom</span> (Lat. 
 <span class="frn ">feretrum</span>), <span class="frn ">eiletrum</span>; <span class="frn ">fomhóir</span>, <span class="frn ">omhóir</span>, <span class="lbl">etc.</span>
    </p>
   
    <p class="result"> 
The lenition, that is, the expunction of <i>f</i> (see 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/&#xC9;riu" title="eDIL Bibliography">Ériu</strong></span> ii 41
      ) 
is sporadically expressed by ḟ from a comparatively early 
period; there are instances in Sg., but the practice did not 
become regular before the Early Modern period. During 
this period fh is also used occasionally (
        ḟh, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/NT" title="eDIL Bibliography">Matthew</strong></span> vi 38
        
      ). 
It is regular in Stapleton (see below). In 
        mórḟeser, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Ardm." title="eDIL Bibliography">Ardm.</strong></span> 
18b2
        
      , where <i>ḟ</i> represents lenited s &lt; <span class="frn ">*su</span> (see above) the reason 
of the dot is not apparent. <span class="lbl">Cf.</span> 
        a ḟiúr, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/LU" title="eDIL Bibliography">LU</strong></span> 10607
        
      . </p>
   
    <p class="result"> 
The `eclipsis' or voicing of <i>f</i> is not marked in the Glosses 
or in the earlier Mid. Ir. MSS. In later Mid. Ir. it is expressed usually by ff; in some MSS often by ḟ, <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> i ḟasach 

        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/LB" title="eDIL Bibliography">LB</strong></span> 276b18
      ; 
        dia ḟaicet, 
          245b52
        
      . In the Late Middle and 
Early Modern period we find ff, ḟf, bf, bḟ, bhf. Some scribes 
use all these indiscriminately in the same text. In 
Stapleton's Catechismus (1639) both lenition and eclipsis 
are denoted by fh.</p>
