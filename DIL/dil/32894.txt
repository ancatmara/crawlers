<div class="row result"><div class="col-sm-3 result_headword"><h3 headword_id="32894">N</h3><span class="citation">Cite this: eDIL s.v. N or <a class="reference" href="32894">dil.ie/32894</a></span><br/><p style="margin-top: 4px" class="small">Forms:
                nin, n, n, N, n, nn, n, n, nd, nn, nd, nn, nn, rn, rd, n, ln, ll, -nl-, -ll-, n, n, n, N, n</p></div><div class="col-sm-9 result_content">
   
    <p class="result">was the fifth letter of the Ogham alphabet, and was called 
by the name nin `<span class="trans">
        <span class="def">ash-tree</span>
      </span>'. It was the last letter of the first 
aicme or letter-group, of which the first two were beithe (b) 
and luis (l), hence the whole alphabet was often called beithe-luis-nuin; see 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Auraic." title="eDIL Bibliography">Auraic.</strong></span> 976
      , 
        1171
      , 
        2806
      , 
        5505
      .</p>
   
    <p class="result"> 
In Irish script n, medial or final (rarely initial) is commonly 
expressed by a horizontal stroke above the preceding letter.</p>
   
    
      <p class="result"> 
<span class="edilb">1.</span> Irish n is of four kinds, according as it is unlenited or 
lenited, non-palatal or palatal.</p>
     
      <p class="result"> 
N is unlenited in the following cases: when an absolute 
initial; in the combination sn; in gemination; after r; before 
a dental (d, t). In these cases, (<span class="edilb">a</span>) when followed by a non-palatal vowel, it is a dental, produced by pressing the flattened 
tip of the tongue against the upper teeth, (<span class="edilb">b</span>) when followed 
by a palatal one, it approximates to the sound of <i>gn</i> in Ital. 
<span class="frn ">ogni</span>, Fr. <span class="frn ">ivrogne</span>. Unlenited n is ordinarily written nn in 
medial and final position.</p>
     
      <p class="result"> 
Lenited n, (<span class="edilb">c</span>) when non-palatal, corresponds to the ordinary European pronunciation of the letter; (<span class="edilb">d</span>) when palatal, 
is a weakened form of (b). <span class="lbl">Cf.</span> 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/IGT" title="eDIL Bibliography">IGT</strong></span> Introd. § 8
        , where apparently `.n. trom' = <span class="ediltr">unlenited n</span> (ceann, corn, coirndearg) and 
`.n. séimh' = <span class="ediltr">lenited n</span>. See 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/PedersenVgl.Gr." title="eDIL Bibliography">Pedersen Vgl. Gr.</strong></span> i 152 § 95
        , 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Thurn." title="eDIL Bibliography">Thurn. Hdb.</strong></span> § 132.
        
      </p>
     
  
    
      <p class="result"> 
<span class="edilb">2.</span> In loan-words from Latin, n remains; if final in the 
Irish derivative it appears unlenited, <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> mulenn &lt; Lat. 
<span class="frn ">molina</span>.</p>
     
  
    
      <p class="result"> 
<span class="edilb">3.</span> O.Ir. nd, medial or final, passes into nn during the 
Mid. Ir. period; <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> bendacht (Lat. <span class="frn ">benedictio</span>), cland (Lat. 
<span class="frn ">planta</span>, W. <span class="frn ">plant</span>), find `<span class="ediltr">white</span>', `<span class="ediltr">hair</span>', gránde `<span class="ediltr">horrible</span>', lend, 
lind `<span class="ediltr">liquid</span>', mind `<span class="ediltr">diadem</span>', proind (Lat. <span class="frn ">prandium</span>), rind
`<span class="ediltr">point, star</span>' = Mid.Ir. bennacht, clann, finn, gránna, linn, 
<span class="lbl">etc.</span> This change occurred early in the case of the art.: np. 

          inda, 
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Thes." title="eDIL Bibliography">Thes.</strong></span> ii 47.24
          
         (Philarg.), 
          indá, 
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Wb." title="eDIL Bibliography">Wb.</strong></span> 20d5
          
        , beside 
          inna, 

            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Thes." title="eDIL Bibliography">Thes.</strong></span> ii 247.16
          
         (
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Cambr." title="eDIL Bibliography">Cambr.</strong></span> 38a
        ); but it is indicated in other occasional spellings in the Glosses, <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> 
          claínn, 
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Wb." title="eDIL Bibliography">Wb.</strong></span> 5b33
          
        ; finnae, 
gl. pilorum, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Ml." title="eDIL Bibliography">Ml.</strong></span> 72b16
        ; 
          linn, 
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Tur." title="eDIL Bibliography">Tur.</strong></span> 109a
            
          
        ; 
          proinn, 
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Wb." title="eDIL Bibliography">Wb.</strong></span> 28c20
          
        ; 

          pronn, 
            31b22
          
        ; rinn (np.), 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Ml." title="eDIL Bibliography">Ml.</strong></span> 145d3
        . The spelling nd, though 
no longer corresponding to the pronunciation, continues in 
use in Mid.Ir. beside nn, and is somet. substituted for nn in 
words where the latter is the orig. form; <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> cend, crand, 
land, mann, rand for cenn (W. <span class="frn ">pen</span>), crann (W. <span class="frn ">pren</span>), lann 
(&lt; Lat. <span class="frn ">lamina</span>), mand `<span class="quote">manna</span>' (Lat. <span class="frn ">manna</span>), rann (W. <span class="frn ">rhan</span>). 
Occas. final rn appears as rd (prob. a mistake for rnd due to 
omission of the compendium for n), <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> 
          ocht ṅdúird, 
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/LB" title="eDIL Bibliography">LB</strong></span> 
63a28
          
         (
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/IT" title="eDIL Bibliography">IT</strong></span> i 40.22
        ) = nduirn (dorn); 
          cartt, 
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/ZCP" title="eDIL Bibliography">ZCP</strong></span> ii 314 x
          
         (=carn).</p>
     
  
    
      <p class="result"> 
 <span class="edilb">4.</span> Medial ln is assimilated to ll, a change which begins in 
the O.Ir. period; <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> élned `<span class="quote">pollution</span>' (as-len-), 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Wb." title="eDIL Bibliography">Wb.</strong></span> 11b9
         = 

          eilled, 
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Ml." title="eDIL Bibliography">Ml.</strong></span> 22b1
          
        ; 
          dun elled, 
            92d12
          
        ; part. 
          éilnithe, 
            <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Wb." title="eDIL Bibliography">Wb.</strong></span> 31b29
          
         = 
eillidi (gs.), 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Ml." title="eDIL Bibliography">Ml.</strong></span> 63a16
        ; do fuillned (fo-lín-), 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Ml." title="eDIL Bibliography">Ml.</strong></span> 26c6
         = 
          do 
fuilled, 
            69b6
          
        ; nud-comálnabadar <span class="ediltr">who shall fulfil it</span>, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Ml." title="eDIL Bibliography">Ml.</strong></span> 46c20
        , 
beside -comallammar, <span class="lbl">ib.</span>, 
          -comallas(atar), 
            105a6
          
         (&lt; comlán). 
Similarly -nl- becomes -ll-: brollach (&lt; bron-lach, <span class="lbl">cf.</span> <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=bruinne">bruinne</a></span> 
and <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=1 br&#xFA;">1 brú</a></span>, gs. bronn), fiallach (&lt; fian-lach), tellach `hearth' 
(<span class="lbl">cf.</span> <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=1 teine">1 teine</a></span> `<span class="ediltr">fire</span>').</p>
     
      <p class="result"> 
Coming between two other consonants n often falls out, 
<span class="lbl">e.g.</span> áildiu (&lt; *áilndiu), <span style="color: #ff8000">superl.</span> of <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=&#xE1;laind">álaind</a></span> `<span class="ediltr">beautiful</span>'; ní cumgat, 
3 pl. pres. of <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=con-icc">con-icc</a></span> (3 s. ní cumaing); aisdís, forgaire, frecdairc, 
scríbdid, variant spellings of aisndís, forngaire, frecndairc, 
scríbndid. See 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/ZCP" title="eDIL Bibliography">ZCP</strong></span> v 1.
        
      </p>
     
  
    
      <p class="result"> 
<span class="edilb">5.</span> Initial n is occas. prosthetic, due either to orig. eclipsis 
of an initial vowel, <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> Dún n-Áis (= Dún Náis, mod. Naas?), 
Loch Nén, (prob. = Loch nÉn), or to influence of the art.; 
for prob. exx. see <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=Nairmein">Nairmein</a></span>, <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=1 nairne">1 nairne</a></span>, <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=nangtha">nangtha</a></span>, <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=napa">napa</a></span>, <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=? 1 nena">? 1 nena</a></span>, <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=noll">noll</a></span>; the word nuimir (Lat. <span class="frn ">numerus</span>, O.Ir. umir) is prob. a 
learned re-formation. Conversely, an initial n may be dropped 
through being assigned to the art.; <span class="lbl">cf.</span> <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=es">es</a></span> `<span class="ediltr">weasel</span>', <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=3 &#xFA;all">3 úall</a></span> `<span class="ediltr">cry, 
wail</span>', <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=&#xFA;na">úna</a></span> `<span class="ediltr">famine</span>', with <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=1 nes(s)">1 nes(s)</a></span>, <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=1 n&#xFA;all">1 núall</a></span>, <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=n&#xFA;na">núna</a></span>.</p>
     
  
    
      <p class="result"> 
<span class="edilb">6.</span> N marks nasalization of initial vowels and initial d, g, 
see 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/PedersenVgl.Gr." title="eDIL Bibliography">Pedersen Vgl. Gr.</strong></span> § 261 fg
        ., 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Thurn." title="eDIL Bibliography">Thurn. Hdb.</strong></span> § 237
        , and 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/IGT" title="eDIL Bibliography">IGT</strong></span> Introd. § 8.
        
      </p>
     
  
    
      <p class="result"> 
<span class="edilb">7.</span> In Mid.Ir. n is oft. used in the formation of adjs. in -ach, 
-aide, abstract nouns and denom. vbs. on the analogy of forms 
in -ach, <span class="lbl">etc.</span> derived from n-stems; such derivations are found 
beside forms without <span class="ediltr">n</span>; <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> bendachtnach, bennachtnach(bendacht, gs. -an); bertnaigid `<span class="ediltr">shakes, brandishes</span>', beside bertaigid; créchtnaigid `<span class="ediltr">wounds</span>' (crécht); machtnaigid `<span class="ediltr">wonders</span>', 
beside machtaigid; mainnechtnach, -naige, beside mainnechtach; mairgnech `<span class="ediltr">lamentation</span>' (mairg); malartnach, -naigid, 
beside malartach, -aigid; neimnech `<span class="ediltr">venomous</span>', neimnige`<span class="ediltr">virulence</span>' (neim); nemnaige `<span class="ediltr">sanctity</span>' (nem).</p>
