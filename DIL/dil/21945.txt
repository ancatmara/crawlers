<div class="row result"><div class="col-sm-3 result_headword"><h3 headword_id="21945">fían</h3><span class="citation">Cite this: eDIL s.v. fían or <a class="reference" href="21945">dil.ie/21945</a></span><br/><span class="revision">Last Revised:
                2013</span><br/><p style="margin-top: 4px" class="small">Forms:
                fian, féne, ḟéinne, Fianghal, Fianghail, —uil, Fiangaile, Fiangaile, fíangalach, Fiangalaich, fian</p></div><div class="col-sm-9 result_content">
   

    <p class="result">
      <span class="pos n" style="color: #ff8000">n</span>  <span class="itype &#x101;">ā</span>, <span class="gen f.">f.</span> By Zimmer (
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/ZDA" title="eDIL Bibliography">ZDA</strong></span> 
1891, 1 ff
      .) derived from ON <span class="frn ">fiandi</span> `<span class="ediltr">enemy</span>' (see 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/RC" title="eDIL Bibliography">RC</strong></span> xii 
295
      ; 
        405
      ); by D'A. de Jubainville, 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/RC" title="eDIL Bibliography">RC</strong></span> xxviii 249
      , and 
Thurneysen, 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/ZCP" title="eDIL Bibliography">ZCP</strong></span> xv 262
      , connected with the people-name 
<span class="frn ">Féni</span>; by Loth, 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/RC" title="eDIL Bibliography">RC</strong></span> xiii 507
      , with `<span class="frn ">fine</span>.' Acc. to Meyer, 

        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Fianaig." title="eDIL Bibliography">Fianaig.</strong></span> vi ff
      . it is from an abstr. meaning `<span class="trans">
        <span class="def">driving</span>, <span class="def">pursuing</span>, <span class="def">hunting</span>
      </span>,' which has in Irish taken a concrete meaning 
and denotes `<span class="trans">a <span class="def">band</span> of <span class="def">warriors</span> on the <span class="def">warpath</span>
      </span>.' Compare 
the mediaeval etymologists: 
        fianna <span class="frn ">a venatione</span> .i. on 
tseilg dognidis isberthi fianna friu; no fianna .i. finedha, 
ar is ina finib ┐ ina n-aicmibh nobidis ... no fianna .i. feinnedha rígh Erenn iat, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/C&#xF3;irAnm." title="eDIL Bibliography">Cóir Anm.</strong></span> 222
        
      . Connected by Wagner, 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Celtica" title="eDIL Bibliography">Celtica</strong></span> xi 265
      , to <span class="frn ">fén</span>. The people-name 
Féni is sometimes liable to be confused with the gen. of 
fian from which it is not always clearly distinguishable, 
 <span class="lbl">e.g.</span> 
        airiseom óc féne, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/LU" title="eDIL Bibliography">LU</strong></span> 5089
        
      . <span class="lbl">Cf.</span> bearla na feinei `<span class="quote">the 
fians Irish</span>' 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Eg.Gl." title="eDIL Bibliography">Eg. Gl.</strong></span> 522
      . Archaic <span class="case n">n</span>
      <span class="number p.">p.</span> 
        féna for Fid, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/PatricianTextsinBookofArmagh" title="eDIL Bibliography">The Patrician Texts in the Book of Armagh</strong></span> 176.1
        
      , 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Peritia" title="eDIL Bibliography">Peritia</strong></span> iii 316
      .</p>
  
  
    <p class="result">(<span class="edilb">a</span>) <span class="trans">a <span class="def">band</span> of <span class="def">roving</span> men whose 
principal occupations were <span class="def">hunting</span> and war, also a <span class="def">troop</span> 
of professional <span class="def">fighting-men</span> under a leader</span>. In later application esp. <span class="trans">the <span class="def">warrior-bands</span>
      </span> under Finn son of Cumall, 
who are described as constituting <span class="trans">a <span class="def">military caste</span>:</span> 
        is e 
ropa thaiseach teglaig ┐ ropa cheand deorad ┐ amhus ┐ 
cech ceithirne ar chena la Cormac Find mac Cumaill, conad 
friusin atberat in daescarsluag `fianna Find', 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/YBL" title="eDIL Bibliography">YBL</strong></span> 325a10 ff
        
      . 

        ro hindarbad Finn ... ┐ ro sgaraid na fiano fris. ar ba hiad-sein cetherna ┐ teglach in rig Cormaic. Is de ad-bertaei 
fíana friu, ar ba siad fenidi ┐ milid in ri īad, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Tochm.Ailbe" title="eDIL Bibliography">Tochm. Ailbe</strong></span> 1
        
      . 
See 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Fianaig." title="eDIL Bibliography">Fianaig.</strong></span> ix ff
      ., 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Keat." title="eDIL Bibliography">Keat.</strong></span> ii p. 324 ff
      . Traditions of Finn 
and his <span class="trans">
        <span class="def">fianna</span>
      </span> are abundant in Irish and Scottish folklore. 
In Keating's time cromlechs were called by the people 
leapthacha na Féine `<span class="quote">the beds of the Fian</span>'. In

iii 280.49
      : bátar fo diamair ... ic fiannaib, ic fomurib (of 
the chief roads of Ireland), and 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Cog." title="eDIL Bibliography">Cog.</strong></span> 114.28
      : i folach fo 
thalmain inā fá dhiamhraibh díchealta ag fianaibh no ag 
sithcuiraibh, the <span class="trans">
        <span class="def">fíana</span>
      </span> appear as belonging to a remote 
past and coupled with semi-mythological beings. <span class="lbl">Cf.</span>
