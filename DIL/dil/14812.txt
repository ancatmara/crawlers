    <p class="result"> 
Dead as an independent word was becoming obsolete 
at the beginning of the Mid.-Ir. period, and words like 
<span class="frn ">deired</span>, <span class="frn ">cenn</span>, and <span class="frn ">forcenn</span> took its place. It was preserved, 
however, in prepositional phrases; but the o-inflexion gave 
way to the ā-inflexion, which latter seems as old as the Wb 
glosses (see above). The acc. sg. remained intact in the 
phrase co deod = mod. <span class="frn ">go deo</span>. The change from the o- to the 
a- inflexion was facilitated by the parallel phrase i ndigaid (i 
ndegaid) which in Mid.-Ir., apart from the slender -d, was 
bound to fall together with diad. Mid.-Ir. <span class="frn ">i ndiaid</span>, then, 
seems a contamination of Oir. <span class="frn ">i ndiad</span> and <span class="frn ">i ndígaid</span>; both 
render Lat. <span class="frn ">proinde</span> (
        .i. i ndígaid sin, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Ml." title="eDIL Bibliography">Ml.</strong></span> 71 b 11
        
      ; 
        .i. i ndiad

      ,

          <span class="lbl">ib.</span> 63 a 7

        mac i ndead a athar, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Wb." title="eDIL Bibliography">Wb.</strong></span> 30 b 14
