<div class="row result"><div class="col-sm-3 result_headword"><h3 headword_id="14561">1 dana</h3><span class="citation">Cite this: eDIL s.v. 1 dana or <a class="reference" href="14561">dil.ie/14561</a></span><br/><span class="revision">Last Revised:
                2013</span><br/><p style="margin-top: 4px" class="small">Forms:
                dana, dana</p></div><div class="col-sm-9 result_content">
   
    <p class="result">
      <span class="pos num" style="color: #ff8000">num</span> (danae only 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/OMulc." title="eDIL Bibliography">O'Mulc.</strong></span> 294
      , <span class="lbl">v.</span> <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=danae">†danae</a></span>). <span class="trans">
        <span class="def">Two</span>
      </span>. 
The quantity of -a- is uncertain. In 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/CCath." title="eDIL Bibliography">CCath.</strong></span> 2239
       and 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/LU" title="eDIL Bibliography">LU</strong></span> 
89 a 5
       it is marked as long; but in both cases the mark of 
length might be due to association with the synonymous 
 <span class="ref"><a class="normal" href="search?search_in=headword&amp;q=d&#xE1;">dá</a></span>; in the latter case all the other MSS, including YBL, 
omit the mark of length. Dana is mainly used in connection 
with the article, which, as is the case before dá in 
Old-Ir., always takes the form in: in dana
      , <span class="case dat.">dat.</span> don 
dana
       = in dá, don díb (don dá). Like dá it is placed 
immediately before the noun (which is put in the dual), and 
followed by the genitive of the tens of the numerals (in 
dana fichet déc).—Probably from †dá n-ái; cechtar in dána 
for older cechtar nái. For the <span class="pos adj." style="color: #ff8000">adj.</span> sense comp. indarna = 
OIr. indala nái.</p>
   
    <p class="result"> 
All examples collected below have been taken from 
MSS not older than the early 15th century with the 
exception of the one from LU. The Glosses contain no 
instances of dana; but it appears several times in the 
commentaries to the Brehon Laws, though Atkinson does 
not mention it in his vocabulary. The way in which it is 
written in the Law-MSS proves that it was but seldom 
understood by the copyists. In all probability the word 
belonged to the late Old-Ir. and the early Mid.-Ir. periods.</p>
   
    <p class="result"> 
The instances can be divided into three groups:</p>
