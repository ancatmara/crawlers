<div class="row result"><div class="col-sm-3 result_headword"><h3 headword_id="23220">fomóir</h3><span class="citation">Cite this: eDIL s.v. fomóir or <a class="reference" href="23220">dil.ie/23220</a></span><br/><span class="revision">Last Revised:
                2019</span><br/><p style="margin-top: 4px" class="small">Forms:
                fomórach, fomoire, fomra, fomaire, fomoire, fomaire, fomóre, fomóir, ḟomorach, fFomór, fFomhóir, fomórach, -aig, fomóir, ḟomhoraich, fomóraig, fomarca, fomorach, fomórach, fomórach, fomórchaib, fomhóir, omhóir, -óir, -óra, -órach, -óire, -óraigh, fodh-, foghmóir, fodhmóra, foghmóra</p></div><div class="col-sm-9 result_content">
   

    <p class="result">
      <span class="pos n" style="color: #ff8000">n</span>  <span class="itype i">i</span>, and <span class="itype guttural">guttural</span>, <span class="gen m.">m.</span>, fomórach <span class="itype o">o</span>, <span class="gen m.">m.</span> <span class="trans">a `<span class="def">Fomorian</span>
      </span>', 
the name of a mythical people entering largely into the 
legendary history of Ireland, see O'Rahilly's 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/EIHM" title="eDIL Bibliography">EIHM</strong></span>, 523
      -
        525
      . They were said to have reached 
Ireland in the time of Partholón: cetchath Herend robriss 
Partholón ... for Cichol ṅGricenchos d'Fomórchaib ┐ fir 
co n-oenlámaib ┐ co n-oenchossaib roḟersat friss in cath 

        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/LL" title="eDIL Bibliography">LL</strong></span> 5a21
       (<span class="lbl">cf.</span> 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/BB" title="eDIL Bibliography">BB</strong></span> 372a42
       = 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Dinds." title="eDIL Bibliography">Dinds.</strong></span> 41
      ). Elsewhere in early 
lit. they appear as two-legged and two-handed beings, but 
generally of great stature and evil nature; Christian writers 
traced their descent to Ham son of Noah, see 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/LU" title="eDIL Bibliography">LU</strong></span> 124
      . 
where they are classed with `luchrupāin' and `goborchind.' 
In later Mid. Ir. and Early Mod. period the word signifies 
variously <span class="trans">a <span class="def">giant</span> or <span class="def">pirate</span>;</span> Giraldus Cambrensis calls the 
opponents of Partholón <span class="frn ">gigantes</span> (
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Top.Hib." title="eDIL Bibliography">Top. Hib.</strong></span> III cap. ii
      ). 
The word is by Irish writers commonly associated with 
muir `<span class="ediltr">sea</span>' (see 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/BB" title="eDIL Bibliography">BB</strong></span> 253a29
      and 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Keat." title="eDIL Bibliography">Keat.</strong></span> i p. 182
       cited below), 
a view adopted by K. Meyer and by Rhys, 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Hibb.Lect." title="eDIL Bibliography">Hibbert Lect.</strong></span> 
1885, p. 591
      ; but by Stokes, 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Ling.Val." title="eDIL Bibliography">Ling. Val. Ir. Ann.</strong></span> 63
      , 
and Thurneysen, 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Heldensage" title="eDIL Bibliography">Heldensage</strong></span> 64
      , with A.S. <span class="frn ">mara</span>, Germ. 
 <span class="frn ">Mahr</span> `<span class="ediltr">phantom</span>' (<span class="lbl">cf.</span> Engl. <span class="frn ">night-mare</span>, Fr. <span class="frn ">cauchemar</span>); 
by A. De Jubainville, 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/CycleMyth." title="eDIL Bibliography">Cycle Myth.</strong></span> ch. V 3
       with mór, már 
`<span class="ediltr">great</span>.'</p>

          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/&#xC4;lt.Ir.Dicht." title="eDIL Bibliography">Ält. Ir. Dicht.</strong></span> ii 6
        , 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Wortk." title="eDIL Bibliography">Wortk.</strong></span> 
86
        , the oldest form of the word is perhaps fomoire, a derivative of *<span class="frn ">fomuir</span> `<span class="quote">land lying towards the sea</span>,' `<span class="quote">shore-land</span>,' 
of which the <span class="case gen.">gen.</span> fomra occurs (
          fine Fomhra,
