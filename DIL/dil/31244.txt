    <p class="result"> 
<span class="edilb">II</span> Mael <span class="pos subst." style="color: #ff8000">subst.</span> is largely used folld. by (a) an adj., (b) gen. of 
a common noun, and (c) gen. of a proper name. 
        a mbōi fo māil, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/ZCP" title="eDIL Bibliography">ZCP</strong></span> xi 149 § 9
        
      . 
        ba leanb óc (gl. maol) Ailill in tan sin, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/&#xC9;riu" title="eDIL Bibliography">Ériu</strong></span> ii 182.8n
        
      , 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/ZCP" title="eDIL Bibliography">ZCP</strong></span> xliv 177
      . The orig. 
meaning in class (a) was that of `<span class="trans">
        <span class="def">cropped</span> <span class="def">head</span>' or `head</span>', in 
classes (b) and (c) of <span class="trans">`<span class="def">slave</span>' or `<span class="def">servant</span>', hence `<span class="def">devoted to</span>'</span>; 
class (c) received its chief development after the introduction 
of Christianity, generally in combination with names of 
saints (in this connexion the meaning `<span class="trans">
        <span class="def">tonsured</span>
      </span>' may have 
been implied; it is occasionally glossed calvus). A humorous 
extension of the usage occurs in the fictitious names 
        Ocmæl-Blongi, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/MacCongl." title="eDIL Bibliography">MacCongl.</strong></span> 77.23
        
      ; 
        Mælsaille mac Maílimme, 
          89.5
        
      . Later 
gilla came to be used in the same way (see 
        <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/ZCP" title="eDIL Bibliography">ZCP</strong></span> xii 301
      , 
        313
       
for discussion of the subject). That these names are not true 
compds. is shown by (1) freq. declension of Mael as <span class="gen fem.">fem.</span> 
<span class="pos subst." style="color: #ff8000">subst.</span>, <span class="case g">g</span>
      <span class="number s.">s.</span> Maíle (a follg. adj. being occasionally in agreement); 
(2) tmesis in poetry: 
        Mael fial Finnia, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/AU" title="eDIL Bibliography">AU</strong></span> 902
        
      ; 
        feil Mail chōir 
Canaigh, 
          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Mart.Don." title="eDIL Bibliography">Mart. Don.</strong></span> Sept. 18
        
      ; 
        Mael caemfertach Cetair, 

          <span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Gorm." title="eDIL Bibliography">Gorm.</strong></span> May 14
        
       Later they came to be regarded as compds. and in 
<span class="case gen.">gen.</span> Máel &gt; Maíl or remains unchanged. Both <span class="case gen.">gen.</span> forms Maíle 
and Maíl- (Máel-) often occur side by side. Maíle is common 
in AU up to the year 850, then gradually gives way to Máel- 
folld. by lenition, and goes out of use by 950. (See Ó Máille, 
<span><strong class="pop" style="color:blue" href="bibliography/Lang.ofAnn.Ulster" title="eDIL Bibliography">Lang. of Ann. Ulster</strong></span>, p. 52 n.
